The case for state-aware breathwork

What compounds, what's measurable, what's felt, and what's defensible. This is an evaluation of an intervention where the science, the user experience, and the market gap align.

00

The Core Thesis

Most wellness interventions fail for one of two reasons: they work but people can't tell, or people can tell but the effect is shallow. The rare opportunity is an intervention where the mechanism is scientifically validated, the user can feel the change in their body, and a measurement confirms what they felt.

Breathwork — done correctly — is one of the only interventions that satisfies all three simultaneously.

But "done correctly" is the operative phrase. The right breathing technique for a stressed person is wrong for a sluggish one. No existing product detects which state the user is in. Every app offers a menu and hopes the user picks well.

The combination proposed here is deliberately narrow: three interventions that share a common physiological pathway and compound on each other without requiring the user to understand any of it.

State-aware breathwork × Exhale-phase entrainment × Self-distanced affirmations
01

What Compounds (and What Doesn't)

Not every research-backed wellness insight belongs in this product. Some are powerful ideas that would dilute focus. The sorting is deliberate.

The three layers
Layer 1 — State-Aware Breathwork Centerpiece

Detect the user's autonomic state through passive phone sensors — respiration rate, inhale-to-exhale ratio, movement restlessness — and route them to the physiologically matched breathing protocol. High arousal gets cyclic sighing. Moderate gets resonance breathing. Low gets energizing breath. The user never sees a menu. The system decides.

A Stanford trial showed that just 5 minutes of daily cyclic sighing outperformed mindfulness meditation for both mood improvement and respiratory rate reduction. But the broader finding — that matching the technique to the person's current state is what matters — is what makes this the foundation of everything else.

Balban, M.Y. et al. (2023). "Brief structured respiration practices enhance mood and reduce physiological arousal." Cell Reports Medicine, 4(1), 100895.
Layer 2 — Exhale-Phase Brainwave Entrainment Amplifier

This isn't a separate feature. It's an amplifier of the breathwork itself. During exhale, vagal tone increases, heart rate drops, and the brain shifts toward parasympathetic processing. EEG research shows increased alpha and theta power during extended exhalation — the brain is more receptive to rhythmic input during this phase.

By concentrating binaural beat or isochronic pulse intensity on the exhale and reducing it on the inhale, entrainment arrives when the neural gates are most open. Over a 5-minute session at 6 breaths per minute, that's 30 exhale-phase entrainment windows, each reinforcing the target brainwave state. This approach compounds with breathwork because they share the same physiological mechanism: parasympathetic activation.

Russo, M.A. et al. (2017). "The physiological effects of slow breathing in the healthy human." Breathe, 13(4), 298–309.  •  Chaieb, L. et al. (2015). "Auditory beat stimulation and its effects on cognition and mood states." Frontiers in Psychiatry, 6, 70.
Layer 3 — Self-Distanced Affirmations Deepener

Across seven studies (N=585), Kross and colleagues demonstrated that using your own name or "you" instead of "I" during self-talk reduces anxiety and improves performance — with almost no additional cognitive effort. Participants using non-first-person pronouns performed better according to objective raters under both social stress and public speaking conditions. Follow-up ERP and fMRI work showed this operates by reducing activity in the medial prefrontal cortex (the self-referential processing region), essentially creating the same psychological distance you'd have when advising a friend.

Delivering affirmations in second person ("You are capable of this") during the exhale-phase entrainment window is a triple compound: the right language form, at the right neural moment, in the right autonomic state.

Already happening in Repose. All affirmations in Repose already use second-person "you" framing — in alignment with Kross's research. This means the self-distancing mechanism is already active in every Repose session. The opportunity now is to make it intentional: adding the user's own name to affirmation delivery (e.g., "You've got this, Gary") would activate the full self-distancing effect the research supports, and the ElevenLabs whispered TTS integration makes personalized name insertion trivial.

Kross, E. et al. (2014). "Self-talk as a regulatory mechanism: How you do it matters." J. Personality & Social Psychology, 106(2), 304–324.  •  Moser, J.S. et al. (2017). "Third-person self-talk facilitates emotion regulation without engaging cognitive control." Scientific Reports, 7, 4519.  •  Orvell, A. et al. (2019). "Linguistic shifts: A relatively effortless route to emotion regulation?" Current Directions in Psychological Science, 28(6), 567–573.
What should not be included
Awe experiences

Powerful research (Stellar 2015; Keltner & Haidt 2003), but a different modality. Can't be delivered through a breathwork session without becoming a different product.

Kindness / prosocial

Strong science (Lyubomirsky 2005), wrong context. This is a solo, inward-facing practice. Adding social mechanics breaks the design contract.

Earthing / grounding

Too much scientific controversy (Chevalier 2012; Oschman 2015), too little control over the experience. Credibility risk outweighs association benefit.

Expressive writing

Complementary but orthogonal (Pennebaker 1986). Could be a post-session journaling feature someday. Not in v1.

One exception: savoring. The post-session "hold" moment — 15–20 seconds of ambient sound before showing the result — is a savoring intervention (Bryant & Veroff, 2007). It doesn't need to be labeled. It's baked into the UX. The user sits in the state they created, allowing the proof to land.

Axis 1: What Can Be Measured

These are things the app can track with real sensor data — not self-reports, not subjective ratings. The phone sits on a nearby surface. No wearables. No chest placement.

Respiratory Rate Delta Primary outcome

Using FMCW active sonar — inaudible 18–22 kHz chirps emitted from the speaker, reflections captured by the mic — the app detects chest and abdomen movement at up to 1 meter distance. The UW ApneaApp research demonstrated this works even under a blanket, with sub-0.2 bpm median error across the 3–60 bpm range. A clinical validation study using FMCW radar for respiratory monitoring in postoperative patients found bias of just –0.12 bpm during mechanical ventilation.

A stressed person breathing at 18–22 bpm who drops to 6–8 bpm after cyclic sighing has experienced a measurable, physiologically meaningful change. Respiratory rate is itself a clinical vital sign — this is not a proxy metric.

Inhale-to-Exhale Ratio Shift

Sympathetic dominance produces short, sharp exhales (I:E ratio near 1:1 or inverted). Parasympathetic activation elongates the exhale (I:E ratio 1:2 or greater). The sonar system tracks this in real time, and the shift from a compressed ratio to an extended one is direct evidence of autonomic rebalancing.

Time-to-Entrainment

How quickly the user's actual breathing locks to the guided pattern. This improves with practice — tracking it over weeks provides objective evidence that the user is getting better at self-regulation, not just following instructions. It distinguishes skill-building from compliance.

Baseline Trend Over Weeks

If the user's initial respiratory rate trends downward over weeks — they arrive at the app less activated each time — that suggests carryover effects from regular practice. This is the hardest metric to achieve but the most valuable: it distinguishes acute relief from lasting change.

What can't be measured, honestly: Without EEG, you can't confirm brainwave entrainment is occurring for an individual user. You can measure behavioral proxies (breathing regularity, stillness) and cite the peer-reviewed studies showing these protocols produce the expected neural effects, but you can't prove entrainment on a per-session basis with phone sensors alone. The entrainment layer is the least measurable part of the stack, even though the research supporting it is strong.

03

Axis 2: What Users Feel

A perfectly effective intervention that feels like nothing will be abandoned. This is where design work matters as much as science.

Immediate Felt Shift — 60 to 90 Seconds

Breathwork produces proprioceptive changes faster than almost any other wellness intervention. Within 60–90 seconds of cyclic sighing, most people feel warmth in the extremities (vasodilation), heaviness or relaxation in the shoulders, and a slowing of internal tempo. These are not placebo — they're direct consequences of parasympathetic activation via vagal engagement during extended exhalation.

The design challenge is making the user notice them. The post-session hold (savoring window) and the single-number proof create a moment of attention where the felt shift becomes conscious.

The Soundscape Breathes With You

When the ambient audio responds to detected breathing — expanding on inhale, softening on exhale, reverb lengthening as the breath slows — users experience something no other app provides. It transforms breathwork from "following a timer" to "the world is synchronized with me." The sound field widens on inhale (stereo width 1.0→1.3, high-pass filter opening), contracts and resolves on exhale (volume –2dB, reverb deepening, filter narrowing). The user feels inside the sound rather than listening to it.

Haptic Pacing the Body Follows Without Thinking

The Taptic Engine produces a breath-shaped curve — a smooth swell that rises like an inhale and fades like an exhale. Not a metronome click. The body follows haptic cues more naturally than visual ones because it bypasses conscious processing. Users describe this as "my body just started breathing that way" rather than "I followed the instructions."

Critically, the haptic intensity fades over the session (1.0 → ~0.15 by minute three). The user feels like they found their own rhythm. By the time the haptics are subliminal, they have.

The One-Number Moment

"Your breathing slowed from 19 to 7."

Delivered after a 15-second hold in ambient silence. The user is still in a parasympathetic state when they see this number. It lands in the body, not just the mind. Not a dashboard, not a chart — one data point in a moment of calm. That's the proof. Every user who experiences biofeedback-style proof-of-change reports a specific emotional response between satisfaction and surprise. This is what makes them come back.

What users won't feel: They won't perceive the sonar running. They won't feel the brainwave entrainment directly. They won't recognize the self-distancing language shift as a technique — the affirmations will just seem to "land" more. The invisible layers are the hardest to market but the easiest to trust, because the user isn't asked to believe anything they can't verify.

The real competitor is not doing breathwork

Most people who would benefit from breathwork don't do it. The reasons: they don't know which technique to use, they're not sure it's working, and the available apps feel either too clinical or too spiritual. State-aware routing solves the first. Sensor-based proof solves the second. The sonic and haptic design solves the third.

Defensible IP

The specific combination of FMCW sonar breath detection + state classification + protocol routing + breath-phase-gated entrainment + golden-ratio protocol transitions is novel. Individual pieces exist in research papers, but no one has assembled them into a consumer product. The transition architecture (destabilize → bridge → arrive using golden-ratio frequency offsets) is already built and working in Repose. Porting it to breath pattern transitions is engineering, not research.

Sensing — Three Methods, One Audio Stream

Primary: Active sonar (FMCW). The phone emits inaudible 18–22 kHz chirps and captures reflections. Chest movement modulates the reflected signal's phase. Because sonar lives above 18 kHz and breathing audio stays below 4 kHz, they run simultaneously in the same AVAudioEngine graph and are demuxed in software via bandpass filters.

Fallback: Passive breath audio. The mic detects nasal/oral airflow sound passively. Research shows spectral energy concentrated in the 200–800 Hz range during exhale events, with median error below 1% at 30 cm. Less precise at distance, but captures breath quality — nose vs. mouth, smooth vs. choppy exhale, approximate duration.

Supplemental: Accelerometer. CMMotionManager at 100 Hz, bandpass filtered. Restlessness scoring via micro-movement energy in the 0.5–10 Hz band. No contact required. Gives the state classifier a non-audio signal.

Mid-Session Transitions (Golden-Ratio Architecture)

If the user's state changes mid-session, the system transitions protocols using the same golden-ratio architecture already built into Repose's brainwave entrainment:

PHASE 1 Destabilize ~30s Golden-ratio offset (φ ≈ 1.618) from current breath rhythm. Gently disrupts locked-in pattern without jarring the user. PHASE 2 Bridge ~45s Fibonacci-convergent ratio between current and target. Both rate and I:E ratio shifting smoothly. PHASE 3 Arrive remainder Clean harmonic lock at target protocol parameters. Full entrainment with audio, haptic, and visual (ISF) layers.

Repose Integration Path
Three Modes

Mode A: Standalone breathwork. Ship this first. Prove the sensing and routing work. Complete sense → assess → route → guide → entrain → prove cycle in 3–10 minutes.

Mode B: Breathwork → Repose. Breathwork becomes the on-ramp. Seamless crossfade into 40 Hz gamma entrainment + self-distanced affirmation delivery. User arrives at the neuroplasticity window already calm, already entrained, already in parasympathetic state. Affirmations land in the optimal window.

Mode C: Adaptive hybrid. If already calm → brief resonance tuning, then straight into Repose. If activated → full cyclic sighing first, transition to gamma, affirmations only when the system confirms the state is ready. User just presses start; the system decides the path.

Shared infrastructure: same AVAudioEngine graph, same binaural synthesis, same golden-ratio transitions, same Core Haptics system, same neumorphic UI language. The sonar pipeline is the only net-new component.

What Might Not Work

Evaluation of risks.

Sonar reliability in real-world conditions High impact

Lab papers show excellent accuracy, but homes have TVs, pets, ambient noise, and variable phone placement. Clinical FMCW studies found that accuracy degraded during spontaneous breathing with patient movement, with limits of agreement widening to ±5 bpm. The fallback stack (passive audio → accelerometer → guided-only mode) degrades gracefully, but if the primary sensing modality fails frequently, the "state-aware" promise weakens. This is the highest-risk technical unknown and should be validated first.

Van Loon, K. et al. (2016). The radar appeared more accurate during low breathing rates with large tidal volumes. J. Clinical Monitoring & Computing, 30, 797–805.
The market may not value measurement Medium impact

It's possible most breathwork users want guided relaxation, not biofeedback. The one-number proof is designed to be emotionally resonant rather than analytically interesting, but if users skip past it, the measurement layer becomes infrastructure without a user-facing payoff. The dedicated breathwork market is also still small relative to general meditation apps — Calm has over 100 million downloads; even the leading breathwork-specific apps are orders of magnitude smaller.

Entrainment claims are hard to substantiate without EEG Medium impact

If users ask "how do I know the brainwave part is working?", the honest answer is "the peer-reviewed research shows it does, but we can't measure it on your phone." This is a messaging challenge, not a scientific one, but it requires careful framing. Over-claiming would damage credibility; under-explaining would leave the most differentiated feature invisible.

Personalized affirmation delivery adds an uncanny valley risk Low impact

Hearing your own name in second person from a synthesized voice ("You've got this, Gary") could feel intimate or could feel unsettling. The research says it works better than first-person framing — but the felt experience of hearing it for the first time requires a moment of adjustment. The ElevenLabs whispered TTS and the entrainment context (user already in calm state) should soften the landing. Worth testing early in beta.

The Shortest Version

Breathwork is the only wellness intervention where:

1. The optimal technique depends on your current physiological state.
2. No product currently detects that state.
3. The effect can be measured with phone sensors in real time.
4. The user can feel the change in their body within 90 seconds.
5. A single number can prove the change happened.

Adding exhale-phase entrainment makes it work deeper. Adding self-distanced affirmations — which Repose already delivers in second person — makes it work on beliefs, not just physiology. All three layers share the same audio engine, the same sensor pipeline, and the same underlying principle: meet the nervous system where it is, and guide it somewhere better.

The technical architecture already exists in Repose. The market has no product that does any of this. The research supporting each layer is peer-reviewed and replicable. The risk is execution, not concept.
