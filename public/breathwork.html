Design Principles

Phone stays put

No balancing on chest. Phone sits on a table, lap, or bed nearby. All sensing is ambient — the user never thinks about the device.

Body first, mind second

The first 30 seconds are pure physical invitation. No instructions, no UI chrome — just an exhale cue that the body follows before the mind engages.

Guidance disappears

Pacing cues start prominent and fade as the user entrains. By minute three, they're barely perceptible. The user feels like they found their own rhythm.

One number, not a dashboard

Post-session proof is a single data point delivered in a moment of calm. Not a stats screen — a felt experience of measurable change.

01

Sensing Layer

Three complementary approaches using only the iPhone's built-in sensors. No wearables, no contact, no special positioning. The phone sits on a nearby surface — nightstand, desk, cushion beside you.

Method 1 — Active Sonar (FMCW) Primary

The phone emits inaudible ultrasonic chirps (18–22 kHz) from its speaker and listens for reflections via the microphone. Chest and abdomen movement from breathing modulates the reflected signal's phase. This is the same principle used in the University of Washington ApneaApp research — proven to detect breathing at up to 1 meter distance with the phone on a nightstand, even under a blanket.

// Signal chain GENERATE → FMCW chirp 18–22 kHz (inaudible to humans) EMIT → iPhone speaker, low amplitude RECEIVE → iPhone mic captures reflections MIX → Cross-correlate TX/RX → isolate target range bin EXTRACT → Phase variation within range bin = chest displacement FILTER → Bandpass 0.05–1.0 Hz (3–60 bpm range) OUTPUT → Respiration rate + inhale/exhale phase timing

Key insight: Because the sonar is inaudible and low-amplitude, it can run simultaneously with the app's audio output (nature sounds, binaural beats). The frequency bands don't overlap — breathing audio lives below 4 kHz, sonar lives above 18 kHz. The iPhone mic captures both in the same audio stream and you demux in software.

Swift Implementation Approach

Use AVAudioEngine with a custom audio unit that generates the FMCW chirp on the output bus while simultaneously capturing the mic input. Apply a high-pass filter at 17 kHz to isolate the sonar return from ambient audio. Cross-correlation between the transmitted and received chirps yields range bins; track the phase of the dominant bin over time to extract the breathing waveform. The Accelerate framework's vDSP functions handle the FFT and correlation math at real-time speed.

Method 2 — Passive Audio (Breath Sound) Fallback

The mic picks up nasal and oral airflow sound passively — no emitted signal needed. Research shows smartphones can estimate respiratory rate from nasal breath sounds at up to 30 cm with less than 1% median error across 6–90 bpm. Even at greater distances in quiet environments, exhale events are detectable due to their characteristic spectral envelope (energy concentration in 200–800 Hz band).

This method is less precise at distance than sonar but has a crucial advantage: it can detect breath quality — whether the user is breathing through the nose vs. mouth, whether exhales are smooth or choppy, and approximate exhale duration. These qualitative signals feed the state assessment and can tune guidance in real time.

Fusion strategy: Run sonar and passive audio simultaneously. Sonar gives you precise inhale/exhale timing. Passive audio gives you breath quality and compliance detection (are they actually doing the extended exhale, or cutting it short?). Together, they form a complete picture no single method achieves alone.

Method 3 — Accelerometer (Stillness Profile) Supplemental

If the phone is on the user's lap or a soft surface near them, the accelerometer picks up micro-vibrations correlated with breathing. But even without direct contact, the accelerometer serves as a restlessness detector. Micro-movements, fidgeting, and postural shifts correlate with sympathetic arousal and give the state classifier a non-audio signal to work with.

The CMMotionManager accelerometer data at 100 Hz is bandpass filtered to two bands: a breathing band (0.1–0.6 Hz) for direct respiratory detection when the phone is in contact, and a movement band (0.5–10 Hz) for restlessness scoring regardless of phone placement.

02

State Assessment

The goal is not clinical-grade measurement. It's a coarse but useful classification of where the user's autonomic nervous system is right now — enough to route them to the right breathing protocol instead of offering a menu.

Input Signals → Arousal Score
Feature Extraction
// From sonar/passive audio (first 15–30 seconds of quiet sensing) resp_rate → breaths per minute (primary arousal signal) resp_variability → coefficient of variation of breath-to-breath intervals ie_ratio → inhale:exhale duration ratio (short exhale = sympathetic) breath_depth → sonar phase displacement amplitude (shallow = stressed) // From accelerometer movement_energy → RMS of high-pass filtered accel (restlessness proxy) stillness_ratio → % of time below movement threshold // Derived arousal_score → weighted composite → maps to 3 states
Classification
State Typical Signals What It Means
HIGH AROUSAL Resp rate > 18 bpm, short I:E ratio (<1:1.2), high movement energy, shallow breath depth Sympathetic dominance. Stressed, anxious, activated. Needs downregulation.
MODERATE Resp rate 12–18 bpm, moderate I:E ratio, low movement, normal depth Baseline. Neither activated nor sluggish. Ready for optimization.
LOW AROUSAL Resp rate < 12 bpm, very still, potentially irregular rhythm Hypo-aroused, fatigued, disengaged. May need gentle upregulation.
Personal Baseline Modeling

The classifier improves over time by building a personal baseline. Your typical resting respiration rate, your usual I:E ratio, your movement profile — all stored locally on-device. This means the app doesn't compare you to population norms; it compares you to yourself. If your resting rate is normally 14 bpm and today it's 20, the app knows something is off before you tell it.

The baseline model is a simple exponential moving average updated after each session's initial sensing window. Stored in Core Data with timestamps, enabling trend analysis over weeks.

03

Protocol Routing

Based on the detected state, the system selects a breathing protocol — not from a menu, but automatically. The user never sees protocol names or makes a choice. They just start breathing and the guidance meets them where they are.

Detected State Protocol Pattern Mechanism
HIGH AROUSAL Cyclic Sighing Double nasal inhale → extended mouth exhale. ~1:2 I:E ratio. Target: 6 bpm. Maximizes alveolar surface area on double inhale, then activates parasympathetic via extended exhale. Fastest downregulation in Stanford study.
MODERATE Resonance Breathing Equal inhale/exhale, ~5.5 bpm. Smooth sine-wave pattern. Nose breathing throughout. Maximizes HRV by synchronizing breathing with the baroreflex. Optimal autonomic tuning at the individual's resonance frequency.
LOW AROUSAL Energizing Breath Emphasized inhale, shorter exhale. I:E ~2:1. Optional brief retention. Target: 10–12 bpm. Inhale emphasis activates sympathetic just enough to increase alertness and norepinephrine without triggering stress response.
Transition Architecture

Protocols aren't static. The system continuously monitors the user's actual breath pattern during the session and can transition between protocols if the state changes. These transitions use the golden-ratio frequency offset approach from Repose's entrainment system:

Three-Phase Transition
// Same architecture as Repose brainwave transitions PHASE 1 Destabilize → Golden-ratio offset from current breath rhythm → Gently disrupts locked-in pattern without jarring the user → Duration: ~30 seconds PHASE 2 Bridge → Fibonacci-convergent ratio between current and target pattern → Breath rate and I:E ratio both shifting smoothly → Duration: ~45 seconds PHASE 3 Arrive → Clean harmonic lock at target protocol parameters → Full entrainment with audio/haptic/visual → Duration: remainder of session

This is the differentiator. No other breathwork app transitions between protocols mid-session based on real-time physiological feedback. And no other app uses mathematically grounded transition curves to prevent the jarring feeling of switching from one pattern to another.

04

Audio Architecture

The soundscape has three layers that blend into a single organic experience. The user should never perceive these as separate — only as an environment that breathes with them.

Layer 1 — Ambient Soundscape

Nature sounds or tonal textures that respond to breath phase. On inhale, the sound field gently expands — a low tonal swell, nature sounds rising in richness, stereo width widening. On exhale, it contracts, softens, resolves. Reverb tail lengthens on exhale. High-frequency content attenuates.

This creates a feedback loop that doesn't feel like feedback — it feels like the environment is alive and synchronized with you.

// Breath-responsive audio parameters inhale_phase → volume_swell: +2dB, stereo_width: 1.0→1.3 reverb_mix: 0.3→0.2, filter_cutoff: 2kHz→6kHz pitch_shift: +10 cents (barely perceptible rise) exhale_phase → volume_swell: -2dB, stereo_width: 1.3→0.8 reverb_mix: 0.2→0.5, filter_cutoff: 6kHz→1.5kHz pitch_shift: -10 cents (settling, resolving) pause/hold → minimal change, gentle sustain, spaciousness
Layer 2 — Binaural / Isochronic Entrainment

Carrier tones generating brainwave entrainment, with parameters that shift based on breath phase. The critical innovation: entrainment intensity increases during exhale, when the brain is in a more receptive parasympathetic state and neuroplasticity windows are more accessible.

During inhale, the entrainment layer recedes — carrier amplitude drops, beat frequency holds steady. During exhale, amplitude rises and the beat frequency can be modulated toward the target state. This creates a breath-gated entrainment cycle that compounds over the session.

Layer 3 — Haptic Pacer

The Taptic Engine produces a breath-shaped haptic curve — not a metronome click, but a smooth swell that rises like an inhale and fades like an exhale. The body follows haptic cues more naturally than visual ones because it bypasses conscious processing.

// Haptic curve via Core Haptics CHHapticPattern { inhale: intensity ramp 0.0 → 0.6 over inhale_duration sharpness ramp 0.2 → 0.5 (slightly crisper at peak) exhale: intensity ramp 0.6 → 0.0 over exhale_duration sharpness ramp 0.5 → 0.1 (softening, dissolving) fade: over session, multiply intensity envelope by decay curve 1.0 → 0.15 // guidance disappears }

Progressive disappearance: The haptic intensity envelope is multiplied by a session-long decay curve. By minute 3, haptic cues are at ~15% intensity — subliminal but still felt by the body. The user experiences this as finding their own rhythm, not as following a machine.

Audio Engine Architecture (Swift)
AVAudioEngine Graph
// Simultaneous playback + sonar + mic capture AVAudioEngine ├── Input Node (mic) │ ├── High-pass 17kHz → Sonar return processing │ └── Low-pass 4kHz → Breath sound analysis │ ├── Mixer Node (output) │ ├── Ambient soundscape player (nature/tonal) │ ├── Binaural tone generator (AU unit) │ ├── Sonar chirp generator (18–22 kHz, low amplitude) │ └── Masking/crossfade controller │ └── Output Node (speaker or headphones) // Headphone detection matters: // Headphones → binaural beats work, sonar uses speaker // Speaker only → isochronic pulses instead, sonar shared

Headphone routing: When AirPods or headphones are detected, binaural beats are delivered through headphones while sonar chirps continue through the iPhone speaker. When no headphones are present, the app switches to isochronic pulses (amplitude-modulated tones that don't require stereo separation) and shares the speaker for both audio and sonar.

Breath-Gated Entrainment

This is where the breathwork app connects to Repose's core technology. The breath cycle becomes the gating mechanism for brainwave entrainment — not a separate feature bolted on, but an integrated system where breathing and neural state change are synchronized.

Entrainment × Breath Phase
// Entrainment intensity modulated by breath phase INHALE binaural_amplitude: 0.15 // recede beat_frequency: hold at current target visual_flicker: off haptic: rising curve (pacing, not entraining) EXHALE binaural_amplitude: 0.45 // emerge beat_frequency: active modulation toward target Hz visual_flicker: ISF at target frequency (if screen visible) haptic: falling curve + subtle entrainment pulse overlay HOLD / PAUSE binaural_amplitude: 0.60 // peak receptivity beat_frequency: locked at target deepest entrainment window
Why Exhale-Phase Entrainment Matters

During exhale, vagal tone increases, heart rate drops, and the brain shifts toward parasympathetic processing. EEG research shows increased alpha and theta power during extended exhalation — the brain is literally more receptive to rhythmic input during this phase. By concentrating entrainment energy on the exhale, you're delivering the stimulus when the neural gates are most open.

Over a 5-minute session with 6 breaths per minute, that's 30 exhale-phase entrainment windows — each one reinforcing the target brainwave state. The cumulative effect is substantially stronger than continuous entrainment at constant intensity, because you're working with the brain's natural oscillation rather than against it.

07

The Proof Layer

Every session captures a before-and-after delta. The user sees one number. Behind the scenes, more data is stored for personal trend analysis — but the post-session moment is designed for emotional impact, not information density.

Primary metric: Respiratory rate delta

"Your breathing slowed from 19 to 7 breaths per minute." Simple, felt, undeniable. Everyone understands breath rate intuitively.

Secondary (stored, not shown immediately)

I:E ratio shift, breath depth change, time-to-entrainment (how fast they locked to the pacer), restlessness delta, session-over-session trends.

Trend Tracking

Over time, the stored data reveals patterns the user can optionally explore: their average baseline arousal is trending down (good), their time-to-entrainment is getting shorter (they're getting better at this), their post-session respiratory rate is consistently lower than when they started weeks ago. This is the long game — proving that breathwork creates lasting change, not just acute relief.

The emotional design principle: Stats screens feel clinical. One number in a moment of calm feels like truth. The post-session beat is designed so the user is still in a parasympathetic state when they see the result — which means it lands in the body, not just the prefrontal cortex. They feel the proof rather than analyzing it.

Technical Risks & Mitigations

Risk Details Mitigation
Sonar interference with audio playback Ultrasonic chirps could be audible to some users or cause speaker artifacts Chirp at 19–21 kHz (well above hearing range). Amplitude calibration per device model. User can disable sonar and fall back to passive audio.
Ambient noise floor Noisy environments degrade both sonar and passive breath detection SNR quality check during sensing phase. If below threshold, fall back to accelerometer-only + haptic-guided protocol (no state detection, just guided breathing).
Phone distance variability Sonar accuracy degrades beyond ~1m. User may place phone too far away. Signal quality indicator during setup. Gentle prompt: "Move your phone a bit closer" only if signal is too weak. Once calibrated, no further interaction.
Battery drain Continuous mic + speaker + haptics + audio processing Sessions are 3–10 minutes. Even at full sensor load, battery impact is negligible for session-based use. Sonar chirp duty cycle can be reduced after initial sensing.
App Store review (ultrasonic audio) Apple may flag inaudible audio emission Precedent exists — several sleep tracking apps use active sonar. Document the frequency range and amplitude levels in review notes. Provide opt-out.
